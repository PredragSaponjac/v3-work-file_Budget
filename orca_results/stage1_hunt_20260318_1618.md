Why this company is the cleanest expression:
Century Aluminum, together with its subsidiaries, produces primary aluminum and alumina in the United States and Iceland. The Company's annual production capacity is approximately 1,016,000 tons per year. Unlike Alcoa, which is diversified across mining, refining, and smelting and is actively selling closed sites, CENX is a near-pure primary aluminum smelter with US and Iceland operations that directly monetize LME prices plus the US Midwest physical premium. Commerzbank noted that "the rise in prices is likely to have provided an incentive to expand production, especially in the US, where high prices are paired with record-high physical premiums." CENX's production profile gives it the highest torque among listed US names to this specific combination of elevated LME prices and surging US physical premiums.

What already moved:
At the outbreak of the Iran conflict on Feb. 28, 3-month LME aluminum futures initially jumped by as much as 10% by March 12 before paring some gains to land around 8% higher, as the effective closure of the Strait of Hormuz has caused a significant disruption to supply. Three-month futures on the London Metal Exchange settled at the highest price since April 2022, after the conflict forced production cuts at key aluminum smelters in the Middle East, which accounts for about 9% of global output. Aluminum price spreads tightened, with spot contracts rising to trade at a premium to later-dated futures on the LME, a condition known as backwardation that signals spot demand is exceeding supply. Commerzbank says supply concerns continue to be felt, with withdrawals from LME warehouses rising to their highest level since spring 2024. US physical premiums are at record highs, adding to the commodity's total realized-price surge. Some analysts are now suggesting that LME aluminium prices could reach all-time highs above $4,000/t.

What has already repriced in this stock (and approximate magnitude):
CENX rose from ~$54 (pre-conflict, late Feb) to a peak of $58.07 as of March 11, an increase of 8.68% from the prior week. However, the stock then retreated: shares of CENX traded up $0.84 during mid-day trading on Tuesday, hitting $56.49 (March 17). Net stock move from pre-conflict: approximately +4–8%.

What has not repriced enough:
The total realized-price increase for CENX is substantially larger than the stock move because it combines ~8–10% LME appreciation with record-high US Midwest physical premiums. More critically, aluminum smelters have very high operating leverage — electricity, alumina, and labor costs are largely fixed — so a 10–15% increase in realized aluminum prices should drive a 25–40% increase in quarterly EBITDA relative to pre-conflict baselines. CENX guided Q1 2026 adjusted EBITDA at $215M–$235M on Feb 19, before the conflict began. The conflict-driven price surge and premium expansion imply Q1 EBITDA materially above that guidance, yet the stock has given back most of its initial move and is only modestly above pre-conflict levels.

Expected tactical payoff range (% move): 10–20%, based on observed commodity leverage and approximate operating-leverage arithmetic for a ~1M tpy smelter, contingent on aluminum remaining near current levels.
