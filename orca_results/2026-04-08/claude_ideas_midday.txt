Ticker | Company | Direction: CF | CF Industries Holdings | Bullish
Catalyst: The US-Iran ceasefire triggered an indiscriminate selloff across fertilizer stocks, but global nitrogen fertilizer supply normalization lags crude oil normalization by weeks to months. CF Industries, the largest US nitrogen producer using cheap domestic natural gas, is being mispriced as a war-premium unwind when its pricing power persists well beyond any near-term Hormuz reopening.
Catalyst timing: 2026-04-08 (ceasefire announced late April 7) | Status: Active
Repricing window: days to 2 weeks
Entry trigger: CF at or below $133 on the ceasefire-driven fertilizer selloff, with confirmation that urea FOB US Gulf futures remain elevated above $600/mt
Invalidation condition: Urea FOB US Gulf futures fall sharply below $550/mt, indicating faster-than-expected physical nitrogen supply normalization; or a permanent comprehensive peace deal with visible Gulf urea export resumption
Target / repricing path: Recovery toward $138-142 range as the market recognizes the asymmetry between oil supply relief and nitrogen fertilizer supply persistence
Horizon / time stop: 2 weeks maximum; exit or reassess before May 6 earnings
Cleanest expression: CF Industries is the purest US-listed nitrogen fertilizer producer. It manufactures 94% of its ammonia domestically using cheap US natural gas, insulating input costs from the Hormuz disruption. Unlike Nutrien (diversified across potash/phosphate/retail) or Mosaic (phosphate-focused, exposed to sulfur-cost squeeze), CF captures the full nitrogen pricing tailwind without dilution from unrelated business lines or offsetting input cost pressures.
What already moved: Oil crashed 17% to $93/bbl on the ceasefire. The S&P 500 rallied 2.5%. Energy stocks fell 5-8%. Fertilizer stocks, including CF, sold off broadly — Finviz confirmed at 11:55 AM on April 8 that "CF Industries shares fall as a cease-fire triggers a broad selloff in fertilizer stocks." CF dropped from $136.84 (April 7 close) to the $132-134 zone intraday (April 8), after hitting $122.50 in Tuesday after-hours immediately following the ceasefire announcement.
Evidence of lag: The S&P 500 gained 2.5% while CF fell approximately 2%, producing a roughly 4.5-percentage-point relative divergence on a day when CF's actual fundamental driver — nitrogen pricing — was not meaningfully impaired by the ceasefire. Oil supply can begin flowing through Hormuz within days; nitrogen fertilizer supply normalization requires weeks to months per Carnegie Endowment, Axios (3-6 months to full regional production recovery), and CNBC (urea supply chain takes months). Urea FOB US Gulf futures remain approximately 50% above pre-war levels. CF's domestic natural gas costs are unaffected by the ceasefire. The market is pricing CF as if it were an oil production stock unwinding a war premium, when its nitrogen product pricing remains structurally supported by spring planting demand (inelastic, occurring now) and a supply chain that even a full Hormuz reopening cannot restore quickly.
Thesis: The ceasefire resolves the crude oil supply disruption but does not resolve the nitrogen fertilizer supply disruption. Carnegie Endowment reports that even if the strait opens, restarting fertilizer production and transport takes weeks — weeks that Northern Hemisphere farmers do not have during spring planting. Ship captains prioritize oil over fertilizer cargo. CF Industries uses domestic US natural gas, so its input costs are unaffected. The selloff treats CF like a war-premium oil stock when it is actually a domestic fertilizer producer whose pricing power persists through the ceasefire window and beyond.
Counter-thesis: CF was at all-time highs ($141.96 on March 30) and is vulnerable to profit-taking regardless of fundamental support. Nitrogen futures markets are forward-looking and may begin pricing in normalization ahead of physical supply. The FinancialContent article notes that CF's stock reached all-time highs "as investors bet on record-breaking quarterly earnings driven by the widening energy spread between the U.S. and Europe" — if European gas prices fall on the ceasefire, that spread narrows, reducing CF's competitive advantage versus European nitrogen producers. Also, BMO and UBS recently raised price targets to $140, suggesting the current level ($133) is already near consensus fair value, limiting the upside available from a mean-reversion trade.
R2 flags: Verify real-time urea FOB US Gulf futures levels post-ceasefire; confirm CF's intraday price action vs nitrogen peers (UAN, NTR, MOS); check European TTF gas price reaction and its impact on CF's UK Billingham plant margins; verify that no company-specific news or insider activity is driving the selloff independently; confirm options flow and short interest direction.
