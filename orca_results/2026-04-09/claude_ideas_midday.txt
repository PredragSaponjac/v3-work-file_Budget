Ticker | Company | Direction: CF | CF Industries Holdings | Bullish
Catalyst: US-Iran ceasefire announced April 7-8 triggered a broad selloff in nitrogen fertilizer equities, crashing CF Industries 15-17% on April 9 despite the structural global nitrogen supply shock remaining physically unresolved. Ras Laffan - the world's largest LNG and nitrogen processing hub - sustained physical damage requiring 3-5 years to repair, permanently removing roughly one-third of global urea export capacity from the Middle East. The ceasefire has not reopened the Strait of Hormuz to tanker traffic; only two bulk carriers have transited.
Catalyst timing: 2026-04-08 (ceasefire selloff) layered on active supply shock since 2026-02-28 | Status: Active
Repricing window: days to 3 weeks
Entry trigger: Accumulation in the $110-120 zone where CF opened and traded on April 9, or on confirmation that the Strait of Hormuz remains closed to tanker traffic beyond the weekend Islamabad talks
Invalidation condition: Urea FOB US Gulf collapses below $600/ton, OR confirmed full Hormuz reopening with verified restoration of Middle East nitrogen capacity, OR CF-specific operational disruption at a North American complex
Target / repricing path: Recovery toward $130-140 range, consistent with pre-selloff levels and the UBS/BMO $140 price targets, as the market re-prices the structural nature of the nitrogen supply gap
Horizon / time stop: 1-3 weeks, with a hard review at May 6 Q1 2026 earnings release
Cleanest expression: CF Industries is North America's largest nitrogen producer. Its core product is anhydrous ammonia (82% nitrogen), upgraded into granular urea, UAN, and ammonium nitrate. Unlike Nutrien (diversified across potash and phosphate) or Mosaic (phosphate/potash), CF is a pure-play nitrogen manufacturer with 100% exposure to the urea and ammonia price surge. Its US natural gas feedstock ($3.62/MMBtu in February) provides a structural cost advantage over Middle Eastern and European competitors whose feedstock costs have spiked alongside LNG.
What already moved: Urea FOB US Gulf surged 77% to $826/ton, reaching levels not seen since November 2022. Ammonia exceeded $1,000/ton, up 20%. RBC Capital Markets noted the Middle East accounts for 35-40% of global urea exports - a larger share than its crude oil export share. CVR Partners (UAN), a smaller pure-play nitrogen producer, rallied over 30% since the conflict began.
Evidence of lag: CF Industries crashed from $133.78 (April 8 close) to an April 9 opening of $113.97 with an intraday range of $110.65-$122.67, a 15-17% single-session decline driven by the ceasefire announcement. This selloff occurred while urea remained at $826/ton, while the Strait of Hormuz remained closed to tanker traffic, and while Ras Laffan's physical destruction confirmed a multi-year - not multi-week - nitrogen supply gap. UBS, in raising its price target from $97 to $140 on March 27, explicitly stated that "current market prices may not fully reflect the severity of ongoing industry disruptions." CF's P/E of 14.28x remains well below the broader chemicals sector average despite 77% urea appreciation.
Thesis: The April 9 ceasefire-driven selloff in CF Industries creates a dislocation between the stock price (down 15-17%) and the physical nitrogen market ($826/ton urea, structural supply deficit). The ceasefire is extremely fragile - Iran re-closed the strait hours after announcement citing Israeli attacks on Lebanon, negotiations have not begun, and VP Vance called it a "fragile truce." Even full ceasefire success and Hormuz reopening cannot restore Ras Laffan nitrogen production capacity, which requires 3-5 years to repair. CF's Q1 earnings on May 6 will reflect elevated nitrogen pricing through the entire war period, with consensus estimates at $2.48/share likely not capturing the full March surge to $826 urea. The selloff offers a tactical entry into a structural beneficiary at crisis-discount pricing.
Counter-thesis: If the Islamabad talks on April 10-11 produce a broader framework and Iran verifiably reopens the strait to commercial tanker traffic, the entire energy-commodity complex will continue unwinding. CF's negative beta to oil sentiment (beta -0.52) could drive further selling even if nitrogen fundamentals remain tight, as generalist funds de-risk commodity exposure broadly. Additionally, US Henry Hub natural gas spiked to $7.72/MMBtu in January 2026 before retreating to $3.62 - a renewed domestic gas price surge from increased LNG export demand would compress CF's feedstock cost advantage, the core structural edge underpinning its margin expansion.
R2 flags: Verify April 9 regular-session close and whether selloff extended or reversed intraday. Confirm current urea contract pricing vs spot (JPMorgan noted contract prices often lag spot by weeks). Check CF forward hedge book and gas purchase contracts. Monitor Islamabad negotiations outcome April 10-11. Confirm no CF-specific operational issues at Donaldsonville or other complexes. Assess whether the May 6 earnings date creates positioning-related selling pressure.
