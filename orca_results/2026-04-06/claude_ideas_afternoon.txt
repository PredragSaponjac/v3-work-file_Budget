Ticker | Company | Direction: CENX | Century Aluminum Co | Bullish
Catalyst: Iranian precision strikes on March 28 destroyed potlines at EGA (Al Taweelah) and Alba, the two largest Gulf aluminum smelters, removing up to 3.2 million tonnes from a market Goldman Sachs had already projected to be in a 900,000-tonne deficit in Q2. A second emerging upstream shock — Guinea's Mines Ministry enforcing strict bauxite export caps in early April — is compounding the supply squeeze on alumina feedstock globally.
Catalyst timing: 2026-03-28 (smelter strikes) and early April 2026 (Guinea enforcement) | Status: Active
Repricing window: days to 3 weeks
Entry trigger: Reclaim and hold above the $57-58 zone, confirming the post-spike pullback is reversing on re-engagement with the dual-crisis thesis; alternatively, a fresh close above the March 31 reaction high of $59 confirms a new leg
Invalidation condition: Confirmed ceasefire with explicit Strait of Hormuz reopening timeline and credible Gulf smelter restart schedule; or LME aluminum dropping below $3,000/tonne, indicating demand destruction has overwhelmed the supply deficit
Target / repricing path: Catch-up toward the Wells Fargo $69 target and analyst consensus range of $61-69, representing 24-38% upside from the current $55 area; acceleration possible if LME aluminum breaches $4,000/tonne as ING and Wood Mackenzie have warned
Horizon / time stop: 1 to 3 weeks; reassess on any credible ceasefire development or if the stock fails to reclaim the $57 zone within one week
Cleanest expression: Century Aluminum is the cleanest listed pure-play US primary aluminum smelter. Operations are exclusively in the United States (Hawesville KY, Mt. Holly SC, Sebree KY) and Iceland (Grundartangi), providing maximum geographic insulation from the Gulf disruption. It is more operationally leveraged to aluminum prices than vertically integrated Alcoa, which has mixed alumina-sale exposure and Middle East customer contracts representing roughly a third of its alumina capacity. Century's accelerated Mt. Holly restart to full capacity by mid-2026 adds incremental volume directly into the shortage.
What already moved: LME aluminum surged to $3,492/tonne — a four-year high — on March 30 following confirmation of the EGA and Alba shutdowns. The Midwest Premium, the physical delivery surcharge for US aluminum, is doubling. Goldman Sachs had forecast a 900,000-tonne global deficit for Q2 before the strikes occurred. Wood Mackenzie estimates 3-3.5 million tonnes of global output is now at risk from the combined Hormuz closure and smelter damage.
Evidence of lag: CENX surged 11% on March 30-31, hitting a 52-week high of $59.84, then pulled back 7-10% to the $53-56 range by April 6 pre-market despite the fundamental supply picture materially worsening over the same period. The smelter damage was confirmed as severe and structural — molten metal solidified in potlines, requiring months of reconstruction — yet the stock retreated as if driven by Trump's "two to three weeks" war-ending rhetoric rather than by any improvement in the physical aluminum supply chain. Analyst consensus moved higher (Wells Fargo raised to $69 from $61; all three covering analysts rate Strong Buy), but the stock price moved lower from its reaction peak.
Thesis: Iranian strikes physically destroyed Gulf smelter infrastructure that will take months to years to rebuild regardless of any ceasefire timeline. This removes up to 3.2 million tonnes from a market already in structural deficit, while the emerging Guinea bauxite enforcement further constricts the upstream supply chain. CENX is the cleanest listed US smelter with operations entirely in stable jurisdictions and is actively restarting idled capacity into the shortage. The stock's post-strike pullback reflects ceasefire hope, not supply-chain reality: even an immediate war end does not rebuild potlines. The market is pricing CENX's forward earnings at a fraction of its historical multiples, reflecting a normalization assumption that the physical evidence does not support on any near-term horizon.
Counter-thesis: President Trump stated on April 2 that US military objectives could be completed in "two to three weeks" and that operations would intensify toward that end. If a ceasefire materializes and the Strait of Hormuz reopens, aluminum futures would reverse sharply from current levels, and CENX — having rallied 60%+ YTD — would be acutely vulnerable to profit-taking. The futures curve already trades in steep backwardation, reflecting the market consensus that the supply shock is temporary. Additionally, CENX trades at a trailing P/E exceeding 110x, meaning the current share price already embeds substantial forward earnings improvement; any disappointment in the pace of that improvement could trigger multiple compression independent of aluminum prices.
R2 flags: Verify Century's alumina sourcing contracts and actual margin sensitivity to rising alumina input costs; confirm Mt. Holly restart timeline and ramp costs; check short interest and borrow availability; verify whether the Guinea bauxite enforcement is confirmed by primary regulatory sources; confirm Alcoa's April 15-16 earnings will not trigger an adverse sector re-rating; assess whether CENX's tolling/contract mix limits upside from spot aluminum prices versus pure spot exposure.
