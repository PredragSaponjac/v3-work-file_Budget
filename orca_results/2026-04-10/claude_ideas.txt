Cleanest expression: CF Industries is the largest North American nitrogen producer with a structural cost advantage from cheap U.S. natural gas. The Middle East accounts for 35-40% of global urea exports per RBC Capital Markets. When Gulf supply is disrupted, CF sells its product at inflated global rates while its input costs remain stable - a direct, company-specific margin windfall that no other listed U.S. name captures as cleanly.
What already moved: Brent crude collapsed 13-16% on April 8 ceasefire news, then bounced 3.6% on April 9 to $98.23. The S&P 500 rallied 2.5% on April 8 and added 0.6% on April 9. Oil is signaling the ceasefire is not producing physical supply normalization. ADNOC CEO Sultan Ahmed Al Jaber stated explicitly on April 9: "The Strait of Hormuz is not open."
Evidence of lag: CF fell 5.7% on April 8 and continued lower after-hours to $122.50 (-8.4% from close), diverging sharply from oil's April 9 bounce. Urea FOB US Gulf remains up more than 50% since the war began. CF is pricing in ceasefire-driven supply normalization that has not physically materialized - oil sees it, CF does not yet.
Thesis: The market sold CF on the assumption that the U.S.-Iran ceasefire would normalize Middle Eastern fertilizer supply flows. However, as of April 10, the Strait of Hormuz remains effectively blocked - 600+ vessels stranded, only single-digit daily transits, Iran requiring permits and tolls, and ceasefire already fraying with hostile statements from Iran's Supreme Leader and parliament speaker. Oil has bounced back toward $98, acknowledging the physical reality, but CF has continued lower, still discounting a normalization that is not occurring. If the weekend Pakistan talks fail or the ceasefire collapses, CF should re-rate sharply toward pre-ceasefire levels. Even if talks continue, the physical supply gap persists: reopening the strait to normal traffic could take months per analysts, and urea prices remain structurally elevated.
Counter-thesis: Morgan Stanley analyst Vincent Andrews noted that equity markets appear to have "fairly efficiently priced in" the nitrogen disruption premium. If the ceasefire holds, Gulf gas exports could stabilize, narrowing CF's cost advantage over global competitors who were squeezed by high energy costs - this is the specific mechanism by which ceasefire-driven normalization could legitimately compress CF's margins even before physical strait traffic fully resumes.
R2 flags: Verify CF's regular-session price action on April 9-10 (after-hours data is provisional); confirm urea spot levels have not reversed; check whether weekend Pakistan talks produce a breakthrough; earnings May 6 creates a hard horizon constraint; monitor whether any interim USDA fertilizer price data contradicts the elevated-urea thesis.
